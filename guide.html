What is PCOS?

PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome) is a hormonal disorder that affects women of reproductive age. It can cause irregular periods, excess hair growth, acne, and sometimes cysts in the ovaries.

Early diagnosis and lifestyle changes can help manage symptoms. PCOS affects approximately 1 in 10 women and is one of the most common endocrine disorders in women of reproductive age.

Causes of PCOS

The exact cause of PCOS is unknown, but it is linked to insulin resistance, excess production of male hormones (androgens), and genetics.

Lifestyle factors can also influence the severity of symptoms. Research suggests that PCOS may be related to insulin resistance, which affects how the body processes sugar and can lead to increased androgen production.

Symptoms of PCOS

Common symptoms include irregular periods, weight gain, thinning hair on the scalp, acne, and excessive hair growth on the face or body.

Symptoms may vary from person to person. Some women may experience severe symptoms while others have mild ones. Proper diagnosis and treatment can help manage these symptoms effectively.

PCOS and Fertility

PCOS can make it harder to conceive due to irregular ovulation, but many women with PCOS can become pregnant.

With the right treatment and lifestyle changes, many women with PCOS successfully conceive. Treatments may include ovulation induction, lifestyle modifications, and assisted reproductive technologies.

PCOS and Weight

Weight gain is common with PCOS due to hormonal imbalances. Healthy eating and exercise can help.

Even a 5-10% reduction in body weight can significantly improve PCOS symptoms and restore ovulation. Focus on a balanced diet and regular physical activity for sustainable weight management.

Gestational Diabetes

Women with PCOS have a higher risk of developing gestational diabetes during pregnancy.

Regular screening and healthy habits can help reduce the risk. Monitoring blood sugar levels, maintaining a healthy diet, and regular exercise are crucial during pregnancy for women with PCOS.

Possible Complications

Without proper management, PCOS can lead to complications that affect both reproductive and overall health.

  • Infertility and reproductive issues
  • Type 2 diabetes and insulin resistance
  • Sleep apnea and breathing problems
  • Metabolic syndrome and cardiovascular risks
  • Endometrial cancer risk
  • Depression and anxiety disorders

A healthy lifestyle and timely treatment can significantly reduce these risks and improve quality of life.
